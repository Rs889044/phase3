\chapter{Results and Discussion}
\label{ch:results}

This chapter reports the final, \emph{measured} evaluation of the proposed
framework. Every number below is produced by the reproducible pipeline
(\texttt{make all}: data $\rightarrow$ features $\rightarrow$ train $\rightarrow$
eval $\rightarrow$ deploy) on the cleaned datasets; none are illustrative
placeholders. Where the measured outcome contradicts the earlier interim
narrative, the contradiction is surfaced and explained rather than hidden, in
keeping with the honesty requirements of a prognostics study.

\section{Evaluation Protocol}
\label{sec:eval_protocol}

All accuracy results use the protocol defined in
Chapter~\ref{ch:datasets_implementation} (Section~\ref{sec:evaluation_strategy}):

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{NASA leave-one-cell-out (LOCO):} all four folds
    (test on B0005, B0006, B0007, B0018 in turn; train on the remaining three),
    each repeated over \textbf{3 random seeds}. Scalers and physics priors are fit
    on the \emph{training cells only} to prevent leakage.
    \item \textbf{Cross-dataset generalization:} train on the full NASA set,
    evaluate zero-shot on CALCE CS2\_35. As established in
    Chapter~\ref{ch:datasets_implementation}, both datasets are LiCoO$_2$
    (LCO); this is therefore a \emph{cross-dataset / cross-load /
    cross-form-factor} test, not a cross-chemistry one.
    \item \textbf{Reporting:} SOH RMSE is reported as the mean over folds$\times$seeds
    with a 95\% bootstrap confidence interval (1000 resamples); model comparisons
    are tested with the paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test.
\end{itemize}

Six learned architectures are compared: a Transformer, an LSTM, a vanilla GRU, a
genuine PIML baseline (Neural-ODE), and the proposed hybrid in its two physics
variants --- \textbf{route~A} (fixed closed-form Verhulst/double-exponential prior
used as a soft penalty) and \textbf{route~B} (a \emph{learnable} ODE-residual whose
parameters are trained jointly with the network). They are measured against the two
non-learned reference floors of Chapter~\ref{ch:datasets_implementation}: the
empirical double-exponential curve fit and a non-recurrent SVR.

\section{Physics Fits and Interpretability}
\label{sec:physics_interpretability}

trajectories (training cells only). Table~\ref{tab:prior_fits} reports the
\emph{real} fitted parameters --- replacing the fabricated coefficients
($a{=}0.0644$, $K{=}1.0000$) that appeared in the interim report. Two facts follow
directly from these fits and shape the rest of the chapter.

\textbf{(1) The two-timescale prior is justified.} The double-exponential model
achieves a lower AIC than the logistic on \emph{every} cell ($\Delta\mathrm{AIC}$
from $-86$ to $-347$, always favouring double-exp), with a higher $R^2$ and a
smaller residual spread on each cell (Figure~\ref{fig:redundancy}). This
empirically settles the Verhulst-vs-double-exponential redundancy question: the
two-timescale decay (fast SEI stabilisation + slow active-material loss) is a
strictly better closed-form description than the single logistic S-curve, so the
hybrid carries both priors and selects between them by configuration.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{2-mainmatter/chapters/redundancy.png}

    it also produces a smaller residual spread. This justifies retaining the
    two-timescale prior alongside the logistic.}
    \label{fig:redundancy}

\textbf{(2) A fixed prior cannot describe atypical cells.} Cell B0006 begins
\emph{above} rated capacity (initial SOH $\approx101.8\%$) and exhibits the
heaviest capacity regeneration. Figure~\ref{fig:fit_b0006} shows that even the
flexible double-exponential, when fit to B0006, struggles with its early
super-rated bulge --- and a prior fit on the \emph{other} three cells (as required
under LOCO) mismatches B0006 even more. This is the seed of the central finding in
Section~\ref{sec:central_finding}.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{2-mainmatter/chapters/fit_overlay_B0006.png}
    \caption{Closed-form prior fits for the atypical cell B0006. The cell starts
    above rated capacity and regenerates heavily; the fixed closed-form prior
    (route~A) cannot track this without dragging predictions away from the data.}
    \label{fig:fit_b0006}
\end{figure}

In contrast, \textbf{route~B does not import a fixed curve}: it learns its Verhulst
parameters during training. On the held-out folds it converged to
$r\approx-0.81$ and a carrying capacity $K\approx0.93$, adapting the physical
constraint to the training distribution rather than forcing a mismatched shape.

The honest reading of Table~\ref{tab:benchmark} is more nuanced than ``the hybrid
always wins'':

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Every learned model clears the empirical floor.} The pure
    double-exponential curve fit --- the ``physical floor'' go/no-go test set up in
    Chapter~\ref{ch:datasets_implementation} --- reaches only $0.0449$ RMSE on
    held-out cells, and the non-recurrent SVR $0.0495$. Both are beaten by the
    LSTM, GRU, Neural-ODE and the route-B hybrid, so the learning machinery adds
    genuine value over curve-fitting rather than merely reproducing it. The pure
    curve fit is also fundamentally non-transferable: extrapolated zero-shot to
    CALCE's $878$-cycle life it collapses (Cross RMSE $0.67$), and its near-perfect
    physical-consistency ($0.995$) is trivial --- the fitted curve is monotone by
    construction, which is exactly why it cannot track real regeneration.
    \item \textbf{The LSTM is the single most accurate model} on unseen NASA cells
    (RMSE $0.0262$), narrowly ahead of the route-B hybrid ($0.0290$); their
    confidence intervals overlap, so they are statistically tied on accuracy.
    \item \textbf{The route-B hybrid is the most physically consistent} model by a
    wide margin (0.918 vs.\ 0.821 for the LSTM), and it generalises far better
    across datasets (Cross RMSE $0.074$ vs.\ $0.111$).
    \item \textbf{The Transformer is \emph{not} the accuracy leader here.} On this
    small-data, leave-one-cell-out regime its high capacity hurts: RMSE $0.0471$
    with the widest CI and the worst calibrated prognostics (CRA $-0.28$). It does,
    however, generalise best across datasets (Section~\ref{sec:central_finding}).
    \item \textbf{Route~A (fixed prior) is the weakest hybrid} ($0.0448$), worse
    than the plain GRU. The next section explains why.
\end{itemize}

\section{The Central Finding: Learnable Physics (Route B) is the Robust Winner}
\label{sec:central_finding}

The most important result of this thesis is a correction of the interim,
single-fold view. On one development fold the fixed double-exponential prior
(route~A) looked best; on the \emph{full} four-fold LOCO evaluation this does not
hold. Table~\ref{tab:perfold} breaks the two hybrid routes down per test cell.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Per-cell SOH RMSE (mean over 3 seeds) for the two physics routes under
leave-one-cell-out. The fixed prior (route~A) is excellent on typical cells but
collapses on the atypical B0005/B0006; the learnable route~B is uniformly robust.}
\label{tab:perfold}
\begin{tabularx}{0.8\textwidth}{@{} l *{4}{>{\centering\arraybackslash}X} @{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Test cell} & \textbf{B0005} & \textbf{B0006} & \textbf{B0007} & \textbf{B0018} \\
\midrule
Hybrid (route A) & 0.062 & 0.072 & \textbf{0.023} & \textbf{0.023} \\
Hybrid (route B) & \textbf{0.031} & \textbf{0.028} & 0.028 & 0.028 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

\textbf{Route~A (fixed closed-form prior) helps typical cells but hurts atypical
ones.} It is best on B0007 and B0018 ($0.023$) but worst on B0005 ($0.062$) and
B0006 ($0.072$). B0006 starts above rated capacity with the heaviest regeneration
(Figure~\ref{fig:fit_b0006}); a prior fit on the other three cells simply does not
describe it, so the physics penalty drags predictions the wrong way.
Figure~\ref{fig:soh_b0005} shows this concretely on B0005.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{2-mainmatter/chapters/soh_overlay_B0005.png}
    \caption{SOH trajectory on the held-out cell B0005. Every model --- including
    the fixed-prior route~A --- sits \emph{above} the measured curve, over-estimating
    SOH as the real capacity fades faster than a prior fit on the other cells
    predicts. This over-estimation is the route~A failure mode: a dangerous bias for
    a BMS, since it makes the cell look healthier than it is.}
    \label{fig:soh_b0005}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Route~B (learnable ODE-residual) is the robust winner.} It is consistent
on \emph{every} fold ($0.028$--$0.031$) and has the best physical consistency
(0.918), precisely because it \emph{learns} its ODE parameters per training set
rather than importing a fixed, possibly mismatched curve. For a BMS that must
generalise to cells it has never seen, this robustness --- not peak accuracy on
one favourable cell --- is the property that matters.

\paragraph{Takeaway.} The ``lightweight physics-informed model'' claim holds, but
the \emph{right} physics mechanism is the \textbf{learnable ODE-residual
(route~B)}, not the fixed closed-form prior (route~A). Route~B trades a little peak
accuracy and roughly $3\times$ training cost (Section~\ref{sec:ablations}) for
robustness across cells and the best physical plausibility --- exactly what a
safety-critical application needs.

\section{Quantifying the Transformer Gap}
\label{sec:transformer_gap}

The interim report claimed the hybrid reached ``85--90\%'' of Transformer
performance. The real, paired comparison is reported in Table~\ref{tab:gap}. We
test at the \emph{fold} level ($n=12$ leave-one-cell-out experiments $=4$ cells
$\times$ 3 seeds), the honest unit of replication: per-cycle predictions within a
cell are strongly autocorrelated, so pairing thousands of them (as an earlier
version did) pseudoreplicates and yields a spuriously tiny
$p\approx5\times10^{-94}$ --- and, because the models use different sequence
lengths, those ``paired'' residuals were also cycle-misaligned.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Hybrid vs.\ Transformer gap, paired at the \emph{fold} level ($n=12$),
with a 95\% cluster-bootstrap CI on the gap and a paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test.}
\label{tab:gap}
\small
\setlength{\tabcolsep}{4pt}   % long first column: trim padding for a comfortable margin
\begin{tabular}{l c c c c c c}
\toprule
\textbf{Comparison} & \textbf{Mean} & \textbf{Transf.} & \textbf{Rel.\ gap} & \textbf{Folds won} & \textbf{Wilcoxon $p$} & \textbf{Sig.} \\
\midrule
Route A vs.\ Transf.\ (SOH RMSE) & 0.0448 & 0.0471 & $-4.9\%$ & 6/12 & 0.677 & no \\
Route B vs.\ Transf.\ (SOH RMSE) & 0.0290 & 0.0471 & $\mathbf{-38.6\%}$ & 8/12 & 0.077 & no \\
Route B vs.\ Transf.\ (Phys.\ cons.) & 0.918 & 0.739 & $+24.2\%$ & \textbf{12/12} & $\mathbf{4.9\!\times\!10^{-4}}$ & \textbf{yes} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

On the in-distribution leave-one-cell-out task the route-B hybrid has a
\emph{large lower mean} SOH RMSE than the Transformer ($-38.6\%$), but with only
four cells the cross-cell variance is high and the difference is \emph{not}
statistically significant (Wilcoxon $p=0.077$; seed-averaged cell-level
$p=0.25$). What \emph{is} robustly significant is physical consistency: route~B is
more physically consistent than the Transformer on \emph{every} fold (12/12,
$p\approx5\times10^{-4}$). The honest claim is therefore ``significantly more
physically plausible, with a large but not-significant accuracy edge'' --- exactly
the property a safety-critical BMS values. Figure~\ref{fig:differential}
shows the per-cycle differential error: positive values mean the hybrid beats the
baseline. The hybrid is ahead of the GRU throughout, and ahead of the Transformer
over the second (more safety-critical) half of the cell's life as it approaches
end-of-life.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{2-mainmatter/chapters/differential_B0018.png}
    \caption{Differential error plot on B0018: per-cycle $\Delta|\mathrm{SOH}\
    \mathrm{error}|$ (baseline $-$ hybrid). Values above zero indicate the hybrid
    is closer to ground truth than the baseline at that cycle.}
    \label{fig:differential}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Cross-dataset nuance.} The Transformer is not beaten everywhere: on the
zero-shot NASA$\rightarrow$CALCE transfer it has the lowest mean Cross RMSE
($0.048$, Table~\ref{tab:benchmark}), with the route-B hybrid second ($0.074$).
This result must be read cautiously: the cross-dataset protocol has only a
\emph{single} CALCE test cell (CS2\_35), so each number is one cell over three
seeds, and the Transformer's mean is dominated by seed variance ($0.019/0.105/
0.021$). The robust signal is that the purely recurrent baselines collapse under
this domain shift (LSTM $0.111$, GRU $0.155$) while the physics-regularised hybrid
stays competitive with a much larger attention model out of distribution; the
precise cross-dataset ranking between the Transformer and route~B is not reliable
on one cell.

\label{sec:phm}

Beyond pointwise SOH error, the models were assessed with standard Prognostics and
Health Management metrics: the $\alpha$-$\lambda$ accuracy (fraction of the life
within a $\pm20\%$ cone of true RUL), Prognostic Horizon (PH), and Relative
Accuracy (RA/CRA). The aggregate values appear in Table~\ref{tab:benchmark};
Figure~\ref{fig:alpha_lambda} shows the $\alpha$-$\lambda$ RUL prognosis for the

\begin{figure}[H]

\section{Ablation Studies}
\label{sec:ablations}

To isolate the contribution of each design choice, three ablations were run. The
design ablation (route / prior / weighting) uses 2 folds $\times$ 2 seeds for
speed; trends are consistent with the main benchmark, but absolute numbers are
indicative.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Physics-design ablation (route, prior, weighting), with a de-confound
control. ``Physics OFF $+$ t-channel'' has the route-B architecture (and thus the
extra cycle-coordinate input) but no ODE-residual penalty, isolating the input
feature from the physics. Route~B is the most accurate \emph{and} most physically
consistent, at $\approx3\times$ the training cost of the closed-form variants.}
\label{tab:ablation}
\begin{tabular}{l c c c}
\toprule
\textbf{Variant} & \textbf{SOH RMSE} & \textbf{Phys.Cons.} & \textbf{Train (s)} \\
\midrule
Route B $\cdot$ ODE-residual (t $+$ physics) & \textbf{0.0196} & \textbf{0.928} & 2.72 \\
Route A $\cdot$ logistic               & 0.0262 & 0.902 & 0.84 \\
Route A $\cdot$ double-exp             & 0.0306 & 0.892 & 1.05 \\
Physics OFF $+$ t-channel (control)    & 0.0319 & 0.918 & 1.51 \\
Physics OFF (no t, no physics)         & 0.0409 & 0.891 & 0.88 \\
Route A $\cdot$ uncertainty-wt         & 0.0445 & 0.886 & 0.75 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Four conclusions follow:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Physics helps --- and the de-confound control proves it is not
    merely an extra feature.} Route~B's recurrent backbone takes the normalized
    cycle $t$ as an extra input that the baselines lack, so a naive ``physics-off
    vs.\ route-B'' comparison would conflate the ODE loss with that feature. The
    control isolates them: adding \emph{only} the $t$-channel (physics-off
    $0.0409 \rightarrow$ physics-off$+$t $0.0319$) accounts for about half of the
    gap, while the \emph{ODE residual on top} (physics-off$+$t $0.0319 \rightarrow$
    route~B $0.0196$) supplies the larger remaining gain, and also lifts physical
    consistency ($0.918 \rightarrow 0.928$). The physics contributes genuine,
    dominant value beyond the input it introduces.
    \item \textbf{Route~B $>$ route~A, at $\sim3\times$ the cost} ($2.7$\,s vs.\
    $\sim0.9$\,s) --- the accuracy/compute trade-off quantified explicitly.
    \item \textbf{Uncertainty weighting did not have the impact we anticipated
    here.} On this small, clean problem the dynamic homoscedastic weighting did not
    improve on a well-chosen fixed weight, and was in fact behind it
    ($0.0445$ vs.\ $0.0262$), the learned $\sigma$ tending to down-weight the data
    term more than helped. We report this transparently: it gently refines the
    methodology of Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology} --- the automatic weighting of
    Section~4.3 is a sound and convenient mechanism, but it is not free, and on a
    dataset of this size a carefully chosen fixed weight is an equally good (here,
    slightly better) default. We therefore adopt the fixed weight for the headline
    models.
    \item \textbf{The best prior is data-dependent.} On these folds (which include
    the hard B0005) the logistic prior beat double-exp for route~A, even though
    double-exp wins the global curve fit (Table~\ref{tab:prior_fits}) --- the
    prior's value depends on the cell mix.
\end{enumerate}

The deployable hybrid occupies \textbf{11.7\,KB of flash} and \textbf{1.25\,KB of
activation RAM} --- about \textbf{half} the Transformer's $22.4$\,KB / $2.5$\,KB
--- while remaining more physically consistent. At this matched size, evaluated on
the same full four-fold leave-one-cell-out protocol, it scores SOH RMSE
$0.0290\pm0.0085$ with physical consistency $0.922$ --- \emph{identical} to the
$4\times$ larger hidden-64 hybrid ($0.0290$), so shrinking from 41k to 11k
parameters costs no accuracy. (It does not out-accuracy the LSTM at $0.0262$; the
``lightweight'' claim is earned on equal-footing size, physical consistency, and
footprint, not on peak accuracy.) All three models fit the M4 budget with wide
margins, so the claim is earned by measurement, not asserted. (As noted in
Chapter~\ref{ch:datasets_implementation}, the deployment stack is
\textbf{PyTorch + ONNX} rather than TensorFlow Lite Micro, and the M4 latency is an
analytic estimate, not an on-board measurement.)

\section{Summary of Findings}
\label{sec:results_summary}

The measured evaluation supports a refined, more defensible version of the thesis
hypothesis:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Physics helps, and the mechanism matters.} The
    \emph{learnable} ODE-residual (route~B) is the robust winner: consistent across
    all unseen cells ($0.028$--$0.031$ RMSE) and \emph{significantly} the most
    physically consistent model (0.918; higher than the Transformer on all 12
    folds, $p\approx5\times10^{-4}$). Its mean SOH RMSE is also lower than the
    Transformer ($-38.6\%$), though that accuracy gap is not significant at the
    fold level ($p=0.077$, four cells). The \emph{fixed} closed-form prior
    (route~A) can actively hurt atypical cells and is \emph{not} recommended.
    \item \textbf{Honest baselines.} The LSTM is the most accurate single baseline
    in-distribution and the Transformer generalises best across datasets; the
    route-B hybrid is the best \emph{all-round} compromise of accuracy, physical
    plausibility, cross-dataset robustness, and footprint.
    \item \textbf{Reported transparently.} The dynamic homoscedastic uncertainty
    weighting did not have the impact we expected on this small, clean problem ---
    a well-chosen fixed weight did as well or slightly better --- so the fixed
    weight is used for the headline models. The mechanism remains available and is
    expected to matter more on larger, noisier data.
    \item \textbf{Lightweight by measurement.} The matched-size route-B hybrid is
    $11.7$\,KB / $1.25$\,KB int8 on the Cortex-M4 budget, about half the
    Transformer footprint, with comparable accuracy.
\end{itemize}

These outcomes correct the interim report's blanket ``the hybrid always wins''
narrative into a sharper engineering conclusion: \emph{a lightweight, learnable
physics-regularised recurrent model is the best-balanced choice for trustworthy,
deployable battery prognostics}.
